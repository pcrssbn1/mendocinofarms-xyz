About Mendocino Farms: Our Story and Values

The Founding Story and Early Years

Mendocino Farms began in 2005 when husband-and-wife team Mario Del Pero and Ellen Chen opened their first location in Westlake Village, California. Both came from restaurant backgrounds—Mario worked in fine dining management while Ellen had experience in culinary operations—and they shared a frustration with the limited options available for quality, convenient meals. They envisioned a restaurant that could deliver the creativity and ingredient quality of upscale dining in a fast-casual format accessible for everyday lunch and dinner.

The name "Mendocino Farms" reflects their commitment to California's agricultural heritage. Mendocino County, located along California's northern coast, is renowned for its organic farming practices, sustainable agriculture, and artisan food producers. While the restaurant doesn't source exclusively from Mendocino County, the name embodies the farm-to-table philosophy that guides every menu decision. This connection to California's farming culture resonated with early customers who were increasingly interested in knowing where their food came from.

The first location operated on a simple premise: create a seasonal menu using the best available ingredients, prepare everything fresh daily, and price items fairly. This approach meant higher food costs than typical fast-casual competitors, but the Del Pero-Chen team believed customers would pay a modest premium for noticeable quality differences. Early reviews validated this hypothesis, with local food critics praising the innovative sandwich combinations and fresh-tasting vegetables. According to the National Restaurant Association's research, consumers in 2005 were beginning to prioritize ingredient transparency and local sourcing, trends that would accelerate dramatically over the following decade.

By 2008, three locations were operating in Ventura County, each refining the operational model and testing new menu items. The economic recession that year actually helped the business—consumers traded down from full-service restaurants but still wanted better quality than traditional fast food. This positioned Mendocino Farms perfectly in the middle ground, and the company emerged from the recession years with a proven concept ready for broader expansion. Menu Philosophy and Ingredient Sourcing

The Mendocino Farms menu reflects a culinary philosophy that treats sandwiches and salads as legitimate canvases for creative cooking. Rather than defaulting to standard combinations, the culinary team approaches each item as a complete dish with attention to flavor balance, textural contrast, and visual appeal. The vegan banh mi at Mendocino Farms, for example, wasn't simply adapted from a traditional recipe—it was developed through dozens of iterations to achieve the right balance of tangy pickled vegetables, creamy spread, crispy vegetables, and properly seasoned protein.

Ingredient sourcing follows a hierarchical approach: local and seasonal first, then regional, then national suppliers who meet quality standards. Produce comes from California farms whenever possible, with relationships established directly with growers for key items like tomatoes, lettuces, and avocados. According to research from the Leopold Center for Sustainable Agriculture at Iowa State University, locally-sourced produce retains more nutrients and flavor because it travels shorter distances and can be harvested at peak ripeness rather than early for shipping durability.

Proteins receive equal attention. Chicken is antibiotic-free, beef is grass-fed when available, and seafood follows Monterey Bay Aquarium Seafood Watch guidelines for sustainability. These choices increase costs—grass-fed beef typically costs 30-40% more than conventional beef—but create noticeable flavor differences and align with customer values. The farmhouse sandwich at Mendocino Farms uses roasted turkey breast that's brined and roasted in-house rather than purchasing pre-sliced deli meat, a labor-intensive choice that produces superior texture and allows control over sodium content.

Seasonal menu rotations occur quarterly, allowing the culinary team to feature ingredients at peak availability and quality. Spring menus emphasize fresh herbs, peas, and asparagus; summer brings heirloom tomatoes and stone fruits; autumn features squashes and root vegetables; winter highlights citrus and hearty greens. This rotation keeps the menu dynamic for regular customers and reduces environmental impact by minimizing long-distance shipping of out-of-season produce. Growth, Investment, and Future Direction

Expansion beyond the initial Ventura County locations began in earnest around 2010, with entry into the competitive Los Angeles market. The Mendocino Farms 7th and Fig location in downtown LA opened in 2013 and quickly became one of the highest-volume restaurants in the chain, serving office workers, residents, and visitors to the LA Live entertainment complex. The Hollywood location followed, capitalizing on the entertainment industry's concentration of health-conscious consumers with disposable income.

The 2018 investment round marked a turning point in company trajectory. Whole Foods Market co-founder John Mackey's investment group led a funding round that valued Mendocino Farms at approximately $85 million, providing capital for multi-state expansion while maintaining the company-owned model. This validation from a respected figure in the natural foods industry brought credibility and opened doors for real estate opportunities in competitive markets. The investment enabled expansion into Texas (including the 600 N Shepherd Dr Houston TX 77007 location in the Heights neighborhood), Washington state, and Nevada.

According to data from the U.S. Census Bureau's Annual Retail Trade Survey, the restaurant industry has seen significant consolidation over the past decade, with mid-sized regional chains either scaling nationally or being acquired by larger operators. Mendocino Farms has resisted this pressure, choosing measured growth that preserves culture and quality. As of 2024, the company operates 35+ locations across four states, with plans for continued expansion in existing markets rather than entering entirely new regions.

Future direction focuses on three priorities: deepening market penetration in existing cities, enhancing digital ordering and loyalty programs, and expanding catering operations. The company has invested heavily in technology infrastructure, developing proprietary systems for inventory management, recipe consistency, and customer data analysis. These investments position Mendocino Farms to compete effectively against both traditional fast-casual chains and emerging ghost kitchen concepts. Gift card sales, catering relationships, and the loyalty program create recurring revenue streams that smooth out seasonal fluctuations typical in the restaurant industry.
